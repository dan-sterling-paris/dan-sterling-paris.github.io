2026 FIFA World Cup — 11 June 2026

The World Cup has started. Forty-eight teams, three host nations, 104 matches — and MetLife Stadium put on an opening ceremony that was impressively slick for something organised by a committee of national federations. The football itself begins in earnest tomorrow, but the day’s content gave plenty to chew on.

Match Results & Highlights

No competitive football yet — the opening ceremony occupied the evening — but FIFA’s official highlights reel covered the MetLife spectacle thoroughly. Eighty-two thousand in attendance, Gianni Infantino looking delighted with himself, and a ceremony that ran to schedule, which is more than anyone expected. The pitch looked immaculate. The weather in New Jersey was, per tradition, threatening.

Sky Sports ran a comprehensive group stage preview covering all twelve groups. Their key finding: Group B is as brutal as it looks on paper. France vs Argentina on June 13th is a World Cup final rematch before either side has kicked a competitive ball in the tournament. Denmark and Morocco make up the group and are not here to make up numbers — both qualified convincingly. Someone decent is going home in the group stage.

Team News & Injuries

England touched down in New York with no injury concerns, which in itself is noteworthy. BBC Sport’s press conference coverage confirmed Bukayo Saka starts the Serbia opener on Saturday — the one selection Southgate named outright. Jude Bellingham is fit; Southgate declined to confirm his starting role for reasons that are obvious to everyone. Trent Alexander-Arnold, who had a difficult end to the season at Real Madrid, got the expected vote of confidence. Given the manager’s track record of backing players through rough patches, that’s probably worth taking at face value.

Brazil remain the most fascinating soap opera of the group stage. talkSPORT’s panel couldn’t agree on whether Neymar’s inclusion is sentimentality or smart selection. The numbers suggest sentimentality: Vinicius Junior has been the best player in Europe this season, Rodrygo has been nearly as good, and their relationship on the left flank is well-established at club level. Inserting Neymar into that dynamic is, at minimum, a gamble. Manager Dorival Junior hasn’t confirmed the starting XI for their Croatia opener, which tells you something. The defensive stats from qualifying — 14 goals conceded, their worst in a decade — are a more pressing concern than the Neymar question regardless.

Talking Points & Controversy

Tifo Football’s Spain tactical breakdown is worth 12 minutes of anyone’s time. The short version: Spain under De la Fuente have built something coherent. Pedri as the controlling eight, Lamine Yamal as the explosive right-wing threat, Fabian Ruiz doing enormous amounts of unsexy work to make it function. The tactical question that doesn’t have a clean answer yet is how Spain handle a deep block — they struggled against Morocco in qualifying for an hour before the substitutions sorted it out. In a knockout tournament, you don’t always get to wait 60 minutes for the system to click.

The 48-team format is going to produce some strange results in the group stage. Three teams qualify per group under the best-third-place rule, which means very nearly everyone gets out of the groups. Sky Sports calculated that a team could, in theory, finish third in their group with a negative goal difference and still advance. The purists are already composing their complaints. They’re not entirely wrong.

What’s On Tomorrow

Mexico open the tournament proper in Group A — the host nation carrying enormous expectation and a squad that’s solid rather than spectacular. England vs Serbia is Saturday; realistically the first genuine test of whether this England camp has resolved its tendency to make hard work of winnable games. France vs Denmark on the 13th will tell us a great deal about whether France’s post-Deschamps era under Didier Deschamps’ replacement has actually changed anything, or just replaced the personnel.
